Enterprise Architecture Performance Self-Assessment

Note: This performance self-assessment is based on the Corporate Chief Enterprise Architecture job description located on the austintexas.gov Web site. The table below assesses the ten duties, functions and responsibilities. The City of Austin architecture staff performed the collaborative assessment. The table uses a 5-scale performance measure where '1' represents poor performance and '5' equals exceptional performance.

Responsibility Performance: 1 2 3 4 5
1. Leads and coordinates the pursuit of corporate technical business information needs, governing principles, and solution architecture viewpoints This is EA's strength. We successfully apply governing principles (i.e., architecture principles) to produce both business and solution architecture viewpoints. Resource constraints inhibit citywide employment.
2. Understands, advocates and supports the corporate business and IT strategies EA collaborates with business partners and ensures architecture solutions follow IT strategies such as Cloud first, use of existing enterprise systems, shared infrastructure and other citywide IT strategies. We also identify reuse opportunities and bring business partners together to share solutions whenever possible.
3. Leads the identification and analysis of corporate business drivers to derive corporate business, information, technical and solution architecture requirements and determines its potential impact EA's Capability Area Architecture (CAA) method is instrumental in the identification of corporate business drivers - reference EA Year End Report. The value of EA reports are currently not realized and the lack of resources results in reduced use of EA process and decreased performance.
4. Ensures governance structure and compliance activities fitting solutions to architecture across all viewpoints The EA Roadmap identified where EA fits in the overall governance structure of the city - see Establish Management Relationships. Once the EA Roadmap is an accepted and normal practice, the performance scale increases. The EA Roadmap indicates coordination of changes with business stakeholders; therefore, a compliance activity fitting to architecture - see IT Project Management.
5. Analyzes current business and IT environments and recommends solutions for improvement This is a significant EA principle and practice - see examples at austinea.org. Maximum performance requires additional resources.
6. Leads and facilitates the creation of governing principles to guide solution decision making for the City Using architecture viewpoints, EA leads and facilitates solution decision making. However, these are not governing principles; rather, they're Architecture Principles. Reduced performance until cultural acceptance of EA in this role.
7. Develops and oversees an architecture implementation An EA strength; however, limited resources prevent maximum performance.
8. Documents and reports all design, analysis work, impact, achievements and outcomes Limited resources prevent expansion of reporting to a maximum score. EA reports out at austinea.org; however, indexing and other referential material is lacking.
9. Develops a promotional communication and education plan EA provides a significant amount of promotional communication and training. An example of promotional communication includes the Intro to Reading COA Enterprise Architecture Artifacts course offered on the COA Train Web site. This responsibility requires successful execution of the EA Roadmap.
10. Develops long term partnerships with City departments and vendors to facilitate collaboration in the development of their and the City's strategic business plans EA provides a method to accomplish department strategic business planning; however, this requires city leadership acceptance - see EA Strategic Planning Meta-Model for more details regarding the EA method.
