In addition to these strategic drivers, the executive team is focusing on a number of other emerging needs for AE stakeholders, particularly accelerating our transformation to a "Smart Utility." "Smart" utilities are characterized by their capability to leverage information, personnel, processes and technology to benefit the customer, utility, and environment. AE has broadened its vision of Smart Grid to one of a Smart Utility, which is seeking to improve its entire value chain from generation source to customer. AE has already taken steps in this direction. For example, our many smart grid and technology projects and plans focusing on providing access to information, easing interactions with the utility, enhancing system operations at reduced cost, and further enabling energy management (AMI, CC&B, C&I portal, MDMS, ADMS). Internally, we are continuing to employ technology to focus on better business management (financial system upgrade, Maximo for asset and inventory management, nodal systems, demand response system integration, energy efficiency, rebate management, BI), operational excellence (mobile work management, GIS), performance management and employee development (TMS).*

PROBLEM STATEMENT

The technology spend across AE is estimated at $20M annually. Given this substantial and continuing level of investment, AE wants to ensure a tight linkage between corporate strategy and technology deployments. To help do this, AE seeks to develop a comprehensive, cross-functionally aligned technology strategy and implementation roadmap/plan for the 5-year horizon prior to the FY 2015 budget cycle.

Creating a utility-wide technology strategy (and executing its subsequent plan) is a significant undertaking that requires high levels of interdependence between business units and levels in the organization. Like most strategy-driven initiatives, its success depends on a shared understanding of corporate strategy throughout the organization.

The need of a cross-functional, cascaded strategic /business planning process leads to insufficient understanding/support of initiatives across department lines. In particular, with technology deployments, planned results have had varying levels of success as measured by advancing the strategic objectives, delivering functional requirements, having sustainable technology support models post-go live, and/or achieving budget/schedule projections.

The process of technology deployment is lengthy. Prioritization of projects is difficult without the strategic clarity, and re-prioritization sometimes occurs based on factors (funding, influence, perceived urgency, etc.) that may not always represent the highest value to the customer or business. This results in business units "filling in" gaps with self deployed, isolated solutions that are insular, not leveraged elsewhere or communicated throughout the company and difficult for sustaining support. This also contributes to an undesirable condition of having aging technologies in production which are never retired when new solutions are activated as demonstrated by the existence of unsupported hardware and software platforms upon which business processes are dependent (example: Maximo deployed for inventory management, but IMS still being actively used by business units on unsupported technology). AE lacks an information model with data source owners, which leads to support, hosting and retention issues. Additionally, without an integrated plan, interdependencies (resources, schedules, integration needs, etc.) between projects are not always understood and managed. Additionally, business unit specific technology needs (where is the work process is unique to the business area) may not always conform to adopted standards or rise to a priority level, resulting in either slow deployment or insufficient support. The business requires flexibility and efficiency in addition to standardization.
